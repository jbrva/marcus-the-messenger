Marcus and the Lost Scroll

A note for parents and teachers

A short guide to what the game teaches, how to play it together, and how to extend the learning if your kid is hooked.

What it teaches

By the time your child finishes the game, they will be able to:

  • Recognize all seven Roman numerals (I, V, X, L, C, D, M) and recall their values.
  • Apply the addition rule (smaller after bigger = add).
  • Apply the subtraction rule (smaller before bigger = subtract).
  • Read mixed-rule numerals like XIV, XLIX, MCM, and MMXXVI.
  • Translate years and small story-problem numbers between Arabic and Roman form.

The game does not cover the unusual edge cases (the bar overline for 5,000+, archaic forms like IIII on clock faces). It teaches the standard modern subtraction-style numerals — the ones your child will actually see on chapter headings, Super Bowl logos, copyright dates, and clocks.

How concepts are introduced

The game is intentionally gentle about cognitive load. Each stage adds only what the next stage needs:

StageNew conceptWhy it's introduced here
I — The ForumI, V, X and the addition ruleThree symbols and one rule is the smallest amount you can build with.
II — The AqueductL, C — still only additionTwo new symbols, no new rule. The child consolidates addition.
III — The ColosseumThe subtraction trick (IV, IX, XL, XC)Now the child has all the small/medium numerals memorized — they're ready for the twist.
IV — The Emperor's HallD, M, plus CD and CMTwo new symbols, plus subtraction extended to bigger pairs.
Final — The Lost ScrollMixed challenges, including MMXXVI (this year)Pulls everything together. The current-year question is deliberately included so the symbols feel like something they could actually use.

Watching the kid work through each stage will tell you which rule needs more practice — wrong answers cluster around a specific rule, not random mistakes.

How to play together

The game is designed so a kid can play alone, but it gets noticeably more memorable when an adult sits with them for at least the first stage. Some things that work well:

Pause on the origin cards. Each stage opens with explanations of where the symbols come from (V is the shape of an open hand, C is for centum like "century"). These are the part that makes the symbols stick. Read them out loud together. Ask your kid to hold up a hand and trace the V between thumb and forefinger.

Read the story aloud the first time. The narrator voice (the scribe, the engineer, the gladiator trainer, the herald) gives each stage a personality. Hearing it read aloud makes the place feel real.

Don't rush through wrong answers. When they get one wrong, the game shows a one-line explanation with the math broken out. Pause and read it together, then ask them to explain it back to you. That two-second pause is worth more than ten more practice questions.

Let them push the help button. There's a ? in the corner of every screen that opens a reference panel with all seven symbols and the rules. Encourage them to use it freely — looking things up is part of how this knowledge gets durable.

Conversation prompts for after each stage

These turn the play session into a quick five-minute conversation when they finish a stage:

After Stage I (Forum)
  • Why do you think Romans wrote 8 as VIII instead of just IIIIIIII? (Shorter; easier to read at a glance.)
  • What number is your age in Roman numerals? (For most kids: a chance to write their own age in I, V, X.)
After Stage II (Aqueduct)
  • The aqueduct in the picture has 150 arches. If they built ten more, how would they write it? (CLX.)
  • What does "century" mean? What about "cent"? (Ties C to vocabulary they already know.)
After Stage III (Colosseum)
  • Why is IV better than IIII? (Easier to tell apart from III at a glance — important when carving in stone.)
  • Look at a clock with Roman numerals if you have one — does it use IV or IIII? (Many clock faces actually use IIII for visual symmetry. A fun "rules can have exceptions" moment.)
After Stage IV (Emperor's Hall)
  • How would you write the year you were born? (E.g., 2017 = MMXVII.)
  • Where have you seen Roman numerals in real life? (Movie credits, Super Bowls, book chapters, clocks, monuments.)
After the Final Scroll
  • Now you can read the year on any old building or movie. What's a place we could go look for Roman numerals?

Common stumbles to watch for

Three small things that trip up most kids in this age range:

  1. Forgetting that subtraction is only ONE pair. Kids sometimes read XII as "10 − 1 − 1" because they remember "subtract" but not "smaller before bigger." Reinforce that the position (before vs. after) is what flips the operation.
  2. Wanting to use IIII for 4 or VIIII for 9. This is a reasonable instinct that the game gently corrects in Stage III. Tell them they'd be right for very old Romans, but the standard now is IV and IX.
  3. Stacking too many of the same letter. XXXX or VV are not valid. The rule of thumb: never repeat I, X, C, or M more than three times; never repeat V, L, or D at all (those are already half-values, so two V's would just be X). The game doesn't drill this directly, but it never shows invalid forms.

Extension ideas

If your kid wants more after finishing:

  • Print the cheat sheet (linked from the home page). It has 16 mixed practice problems with an answer key — perfect for a car ride or a five-minute warm-up.
  • Roman numeral year hunt. Walk around your neighborhood (or any old downtown) and look for cornerstones, plaques, or building dates. Bring a notebook.
  • Movie credits scavenger hunt. Pause the credits at the end of any film and find the copyright year. Read it together.
  • Build them with sticks. For tactile learners, try forming numerals out of toothpicks, popsicle sticks, or even drawing them in sand.
  • Roman addition puzzles. Give them two numerals (e.g., XIV + XXII) and have them convert, add, and convert back.
  • Bigger numbers. Romans used a bar over a numeral to multiply by 1,000 (so V̄ = 5,000). This isn't in the game, but it's a fun "next step" if they've mastered the basics.

A note on the game's tech

The game is a single HTML file that runs entirely in a browser. No internet is needed after the first time it loads (it caches the fonts). It saves your child's progress in their browser, so closing the tab won't lose their place — but be aware that "clear browsing data" will reset progress, and progress doesn't sync between devices. If multiple kids will play on the same computer, each one will need to finish their playthrough or click "Start Over" before the next.

If your child finishes and asks for more — or if you'd like to see other stages added (medieval clocks, modern uses, harder challenges) — those are listed as next-step ideas in the project documentation.
